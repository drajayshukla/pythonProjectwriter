Microarchitectural Deterioration and Reduced Bone Strength in MASLD: An HR-pQCT Study

Author

Dr. Ajay Shukla, et al.

Published

February 14, 2026

Lay Summary

Patients with fatty liver disease and advanced scarring (fibrosis) have significantly weaker bones despite often being heavier in weight. Standard bone scans (DXA) miss this weakness because the damage happens inside the bone’s outer shell. Our study using high-resolution imaging shows that a liver-produced protein called Sclerostin may be causing this “hollowing out” of the bone—a process known as trabecularization—leading to a high risk of fractures that traditional tests overlook.

Highlights

  • The BMI Paradox: Advanced fibrosis in MASLD is associated with severe cortical bone deterioration despite a significantly higher BMI (\(28.7\) vs \(24.4\) kg/m²).
  • Cortical “Trabecularization”: HR-pQCT revealed a 96% increase in cortical porosity (\(Ct.Po\)) in patients with advanced fibrosis compared to healthy controls.
  • Failure Load Deficit: Micro-Finite Element Analysis (\(\mu\)FEA) demonstrated a marked reduction in bone failure load (3212 N vs 3816 N), highlighting a metabolic “insult” that overrides mechanical loading protection.
  • Biochemical Mediator: Elevated serum Sclerostin (\(671.7\) vs \(387.7\) pg/mL) was identified as a primary mechanistic link in the hollowing of the cortical shell.

Abstract

Introduction: Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD) impacts the “liver-bone axis.” We aimed to evaluate bone strength and microarchitecture in patients with advanced fibrosis using HR-pQCT and \(\mu\)FEA.

Methods: A cross-sectional study of 100 subjects (Advanced Fibrosis [LSM \(\ge\) 12 kPa] vs. Controls [LSM < 6 kPa]) was conducted. Bone parameters were assessed via distal radius HR-pQCT; serum Sclerostin and IGF-1 were measured via ELISA.

Results: Patients with advanced fibrosis exhibited a significantly higher BMI (\(28.74 \pm 6.05\) vs. \(24.42 \pm 4.74\) kg/m², \(p < 0.001\)) but demonstrated a significant increase in Radius Cortical Porosity (\(5.49 \pm 1.84\%\) vs. \(2.80 \pm 0.89\%\), \(p < 0.001\)). FEA-derived Failure Load was significantly lower in the fibrosis group (\(3212 \pm 645\) N vs. \(3816 \pm 342\) N, \(p < 0.001\)). Sclerostin levels were significantly elevated (\(671.7 \pm 170.5\) vs. \(387.7 \pm 70.1\) pg/mL, \(p < 0.001\)), while IGF-1 was reduced (\(90.8\) vs \(162.1\) ng/mL).

Conclusion: Advanced MASLD drives a compartment-specific decay of cortical bone that overrides the protective effects of increased BMI. HR-pQCT identifies skeletal fragility missed by traditional densitometry.

Introduction

Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD) has emerged as a multi-systemic disorder where hepatic dysfunction triggers a state of skeletal uncoupling. Factors such as IGF-1 deficiency and sclerostin excess disrupt the bone multicellular unit (BMU) (Hu and Zhang 2023; Moonen et al. 2022).

Standard assessment using DXA often fails to capture the fracture risk in MASLD, particularly when obesity-related mechanical loading masks underlying fragility. We hypothesized that advanced MASLD drives a cortical “trabecularization” that overrides mechanical protection.

Methods

Participant Selection

A cross-sectional study included \(N=100\) subjects at a tertiary care center in Lucknow. Advanced fibrosis (F3-F4) was defined by \(LSM \ge 12\) kPa via FibroScan. Controls (\(LSM < 6\) kPa) were age-matched. Exclusion criteria included CKD (eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m²), Vitamin D deficiency (< 20 ng/mL), and history of osteo-active medications.

Imaging and Biomechanics

Distal radius scans were acquired via HR-pQCT (XtremeCT II) at 61 \(\mu\)m resolution. Micro-Finite Element Analysis (\(\mu\)FEA) simulated uniaxial compression to estimate Failure Load (\(F_f\), N) and stiffness. Serum Sclerostin and IGF-1 were quantified via high-sensitivity ELISA.

Statistical Analysis

Data were analyzed using Python 3.11 (Pandas/SciPy). Group comparisons were performed using independent T-tests. A multivariate linear regression model adjusted for BMI and age to evaluate the independent association between LSM and cortical porosity.

Results

The study cohorts were well-matched for age (\(p = 0.090\)), but patients with advanced fibrosis exhibited a significantly higher BMI (\(28.74 \pm 6.05\) vs. \(24.42 \pm 4.74\) kg/m², \(p < 0.001\)). Biochemically, serum Sclerostin levels were significantly higher in the fibrosis cohort (\(671.74 \pm 170.51\) vs. \(387.74 \pm 70.09\) pg/mL, \(p < 0.001\)), whereas IGF-1 levels were significantly lower (\(90.76 \pm 21.55\) vs. \(162.10 \pm 29.35\) ng/mL, \(p < 0.001\)).

HR-pQCT analysis revealed a 96% increase in Radius Cortical Porosity (\(Ct.Po\)) in the F3-F4 group (\(5.49 \pm 1.84\%\) vs. \(2.80 \pm 0.89\%\), \(p < 0.001\)). This structural “hollowing” resulted in a significant reduction in estimated Failure Load (\(3212.12 \pm 644.90\) N vs. \(3815.94 \pm 342.13\) N, \(p < 0.001\)).

Multivariate linear regression confirmed that Liver Stiffness (LSM) was an independent predictor of \(Ct.Po\) (\(\beta = 0.15\), \(p < 0.001\)), even after adjusting for the confounding effects of BMI and age (\(R^2 = 0.48\)).

Discussion and Conclusion

This study confirms that advanced liver fibrosis is associated with a distinct skeletal phenotype characterized by cortical deterioration. The key finding—marked cortical porosity despite significantly higher BMI—suggests that metabolic toxicity overrides mechanical loading principles. Elevated sclerostin provides a mechanistic link, acting as a Wnt-pathway antagonist that drives the “trabecularization” of the cortex. Clinicians should consider advanced bone assessment in MASLD patients regardless of BMI or DXA scores.
